# Penguin's 100 Must-Read Classics (As Chosen by Readers)

*Note: This list actually contains 99 books, as #27 was accidentally skipped in the original publication.*

| # | Title | Author | Year | Description |
|---|-------|--------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Pride and Prejudice | Jane Austen | 1813 | A sparkling comedy of manners following Elizabeth Bennet as she navigates love, class, and family expectations in Regency England. The witty romance explores themes of first impressions, prejudice, and personal growth. |
| 2 | To Kill a Mockingbird | Harper Lee | 1960 | Set in Depression-era Alabama, this Pulitzer Prize winner addresses racism and injustice through the eyes of young Scout Finch as her father defends a Black man falsely accused of rape. |
| 3 | The Great Gatsby | F. Scott Fitzgerald | 1925 | A portrait of the Jazz Age following mysterious millionaire Jay Gatsby and his obsession with the beautiful Daisy Buchanan, exploring themes of wealth, love, and the American Dream. |
| 4 | One Hundred Years of Solitude | Gabriel García Márquez | 1967 | A landmark work of magical realism chronicling seven generations of the Buendía family in the fictional town of Macondo, blending fantasy and reality. |
| 5 | In Cold Blood | Truman Capote | 1965 | A pioneering work of true crime literature detailing the brutal 1959 murder of the Clutter family in Kansas and the investigation that followed. |
| 6 | Wide Sargasso Sea | Jean Rhys | 1966 | A postcolonial prequel to Jane Eyre, telling the story of Bertha Mason (the "madwoman in the attic") and her life in Jamaica before her marriage to Rochester. |
| 7 | Brave New World | Aldous Huxley | 1932 | A dystopian novel depicting a futuristic World State where citizens are genetically modified and conditioned for social stability, questioning the cost of progress. |
| 8 | I Capture The Castle | Dodie Smith | 1948 | A charming coming-of-age story about seventeen-year-old Cassandra Mortmain and her eccentric family living in a dilapidated English castle. |
| 9 | Jane Eyre | Charlotte Brontë | 1847 | The story of an orphaned governess who falls in love with her employer, Mr. Rochester, while asserting her independence and moral strength. |
| 10 | Crime and Punishment | Fyodor Dostoevsky | 1866 | A psychological thriller following impoverished student Raskolnikov who commits murder and grapples with guilt, redemption, and moral philosophy. |
| 11 | The Secret History | Donna Tartt | 1992 | A contemporary classic about a group of elite college students who become entangled in murder, exploring themes of beauty, terror, and moral ambiguity. |
| 12 | The Call of the Wild | Jack London | 1903 | An adventure novel about Buck, a domesticated dog who is stolen and sold as a sled dog in the Yukon, gradually reverting to his wild instincts. |
| 13 | The Chrysalids | John Wyndham | 1955 | A post-apocalyptic science fiction novel about a society that ruthlessly purges genetic mutations, following a boy with telepathic abilities. |
| 14 | Persuasion | Jane Austen | 1818 | Austen's final completed novel about Anne Elliot, who gets a second chance at love with Captain Wentworth eight years after she was persuaded to reject him. |
| 15 | Moby-Dick | Herman Melville | 1851 | An epic tale of Captain Ahab's obsessive quest to hunt the white whale that maimed him, exploring themes of fate, nature, and obsession. |
| 16 | The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe | C.S. Lewis | 1950 | The first published book in the Chronicles of Narnia series, where four children discover a magical world through a wardrobe. |
| 17 | To the Lighthouse | Virginia Woolf | 1927 | A modernist novel examining the Ramsay family's visits to the Isle of Skye, exploring memory, time, and the nature of perception. |
| 18 | The Death of the Heart | Elizabeth Bowen | 1938 | A sensitive portrayal of sixteen-year-old Portia's experiences living with her half-brother and his wife in London, exploring innocence and betrayal. |
| 19 | Tess of the d'Urbervilles | Thomas Hardy | 1891 | A tragic tale of a young woman from a poor family who suffers exploitation and hardship in Victorian England. |
| 20 | Frankenstein | Mary Shelley | 1818 | The gothic tale of Victor Frankenstein and his creation, exploring themes of ambition, responsibility, and what it means to be human. |
| 21 | The Master and Margarita | Mikhail Bulgakov | 1966 | A satirical fantasy novel about the devil visiting Soviet Moscow, interweaving stories of Pontius Pilate and a doomed romance. |
| 22 | The Go-Between | L.P. Hartley | 1953 | A tale of forbidden love set in the English countryside, famous for its opening line: "The past is a foreign country: they do things differently there." |
| 23 | One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest | Ken Kesey | 1962 | Set in a psychiatric hospital, following rebellious Randle McMurphy's battle against institutional authority and conformity. |
| 24 | Nineteen Eighty-Four | George Orwell | 1949 | A dystopian masterpiece about totalitarianism, surveillance, and the manipulation of truth in a nightmarish future society. |
| 25 | Buddenbrooks | Thomas Mann | 1901 | The decline of a wealthy merchant family over four generations in 19th-century Germany, examining the conflict between art and commerce. |
| 26 | The Grapes of Wrath | John Steinbeck | 1939 | An American classic following the Joad family's migration from the Dust Bowl to California during the Great Depression. |
| 28 | The Code of the Woosters | P.G. Wodehouse | 1938 | A comic novel featuring the hapless Bertie Wooster and his ingenious valet Jeeves in another hilarious escapade. |
| 29 | Dracula | Bram Stoker | 1897 | The quintessential vampire novel told through letters and diary entries, following Count Dracula's attempt to move to England. |
| 30 | The Lord of the Rings | J.R.R. Tolkien | 1954 | An epic fantasy trilogy about hobbit Frodo Baggins's quest to destroy a powerful ring and defeat the dark lord Sauron. |
| 31 | The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn | Mark Twain | 1884 | A satirical novel following young Huck Finn and escaped slave Jim as they travel down the Mississippi River. |
| 32 | Great Expectations | Charles Dickens | 1860 | The coming-of-age story of orphan Pip and his experiences with wealth, poverty, love, and moral redemption. |
| 33 | Catch-22 | Joseph Heller | 1961 | A satirical war novel about the absurdity of military bureaucracy during World War II, featuring the paradoxical "Catch-22" rule. |
| 34 | The Age of Innocence | Edith Wharton | 1920 | A portrait of upper-class New York society in the 1870s, exploring social expectations and forbidden love. |
| 35 | Things Fall Apart | Chinua Achebe | 1958 | A landmark African novel depicting the collision of traditional Igbo society with European colonialism. |
| 36 | Middlemarch | George Eliot | 1871 | An ambitious novel about provincial English life, following multiple characters navigating marriage, politics, and social change. |
